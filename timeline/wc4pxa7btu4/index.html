I got to attend. I was reminded of this when, yesterday, I was talking about not going to Fallout 2’s ship party, and it made me go look up my notes, because I know I went to Fallout 3s and it was an amazing party and I will tell about it. We even got some pictures, but the reason I’m wearing my glasses today is I found my notes and, rather than talk about those two things, I want to read you my notes on them because I want to put you into the headspace I was in. These were written back at the time and so I will start with the dates on them. I thought was very cool that I got. Invited to these things, so once again I’m going to thank Todd Howard and Pete Hines for extending this invitation. So first let me start with the Fallout 3 E3 showing.

On July 13th 2007, I drove to Santa Monica where that Year’s Electronic Entertainment Expo was being held in a hanger at the S Sant Monica Airport and in various hotels around the city. I headed down to the Beachside hotel called The Fairmont, where Bethesda had set up some private showings of Fallout 3 on the Xbox 360. I got to see a 45-minute demo of the game and then I headed to lunch with lead designer Amo pag Rulo I — I’m sorry if I mess up that name — executive producer Todd Howard and their vice president, PR, Pete Hines. We geeked out for an hour talking about the game and its predecessors. I liked what I saw and I told them when.

Leonard and Jason and I had imagined making Fallout into a 3D game back in ‘94, we imagined it looking very similar to what I saw. That day, 10 years after Fallout shipped, I was seeing that dream made into reality. I was really excited, somewhat jealous and a little sad.

Fallout was not mine anymore. Many people would argue it was never mind and they would be correct in a legal sense and wrong in every other way. So that’s what I wrote in July of 2007. So let’s Flash Forward to October of 2008 and I will tell you about the Fallout 3 launch party. Bethesda threw a huge launch party for Fallout 3 on Thursday October 16th 2008 at 9 pm at the L Los Angeles center studios, which was a big movie studio and TV Film Studio in downtown Los Angeles.

It’s a studio where Madmen was filmed, if you. Want a visual of the environment.

The studio had six huge sound stages, a theater, a big Club room and a huge outdoor deck. The place was about four city blocks in size, so the Foo Fighters concert held later that evening was simply held on a one street that they had roped off and erected a stage on. There were four separate parking lots, not including the VIP underground lot with its private entrance on a different street. I was not issued a VIP ticket, so I wandered in from the entrance that was nearest to my hotel.

This was a serious party. I entered on a literal red carpet.

The photographers — about 20 or so — looked up, Shrugged and went back to talking among themselves. About a minute later, Lauren Conrad from the hills showed up behind me and there was serious jostling among photographers to get her picture. I didn’t recognize her until they started calling out her name, trying to get her to look directly at the cameras.

Along the red carpet were a couple big statues of power armor, similar to the ones in Fallout, but not quite the same. So I’d guess the Bethesda artist had tweaked the design a bit. There was also a big Airstream trailer surrounded by The Fallout 3 nuclear family, which were mannequins and Vault suits. Bethesda was also projecting The Fallout 3 Logo onto nearby Office Buildings at about the 10th or 15th story. So the logos were huge and would animate across the building faces. So no matter where you stood at the party, you could always see the logos moving out of the corner of your eye. You could not forget what party you were at and I was sure this was the intended effect. I arrived in time to hear the Ink Spots playing on a stage.

Near the first bar. The original inkpot members had passed away by 2008, but the lead singer was the son of the original member. I talked to them after their set.

Speaking of bars, there were about 15 bars scattered all over the area. All of them were opened and all of them were very well stocked. I got a drink and went back to listen to The Ink Spots and then I noticed that some of the Fallout 3 development team was nearby. I chatted again with lead designer Emil pagliarulo, uh, lead programmer Steve meister and a few artists and then most of them want walked away, but I was hanging out with Steve for most of the rest of the evening. At this point around 10: pm, it was very crowded. I estimated that there was about 1500 to 2,000 people at this party. This was a serious party. The lead programmer, Steve, and I wandered around the club for about an hour. Steve was as amazed as I was at the sheer size and obvious expense of this party.

There were Fallout decorations everywhere, from flat screens on the wall showing the new artwork to oldfashioned black and white TV showing the 50s style Vault commercials. There were three stations of four televisions, each one dressed up as a radiation King brand television from the opening of Fallout — and, by the way, it’s also the same brand that Homer watched as a child in The Simpsons. You could play Fallout 3 on them as long as you liked, while waitresses brought a seemingly endless array of pre-made drinks and snacks. I didn’t drink too much alcoholic stuff that night. I tried something like a hot bloody mary that was very strong, and then a different drink that was warm and frothy and milky. There were so many bars. That none of them had long lines.

A few times I went to get a soda or water. I was shocked at how many people were there and how much they were drinking. I could not imagine what this party cost, just from the food, drinks and studio rental alone. This was a very serious party, I read. Yeah, this is where you can call me old man Kane. I stayed at the party till about 1:00 in the morning, so I was there for about 4 hours. I said goodbye to Steve and I st back to the studio, tired and just a little bit tipsy. As I walked back to the hotel, I had time to think about how strange the path was that led me here. When I started Fallout 14 years before, it was me alone in a room trying to get Sprites to move along a hex grid that was displayed isometrically. Now I was leaving a star studded Los Angeles party filled with people who had no idea who I was but seemed very happy with the new Fallout 3 game. I guess I felt happy that the Fallout IP was not collecting dust in a file cabinet somewhere like Arcanum was and still is, but instead a whole new generation of Gamers were going to be exposed to Fallout for the first time, and there it was a nagging part of.
