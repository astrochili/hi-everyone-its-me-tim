As for your question on utilities, my I’ll tell you what my goal was. Every magic college was supposed to have, at the very minimum, a spell that was useful in combat, that either did damage or affected a combatant some way, so they did less damage or something. And then a utility spell, which was a utility, was defined as it helped you in exploration. You know, maybe it helped you climb up something or un — or find a trap or open a lock or just avoid combat altogether by going invisible or, you know, teleporting past something, Um any kind of even healing. General support, all of those spells, and then I also wanted every college to have what I called a showcase spell, which was something aspirational, that when you were looking at the college list you went, “I really have to take that college, That’s the spell I want to get”.

Usually it was the highest one, It was level five, but not always. I don’t know if I achieved that goal. I would say no, but that was what I was trying to do And if I could go back, I would probably adjust, change some spells and balance others to try to achieve that goal. I also really, really wanted to make it so that if your build was a generalist, you dabbled in at many of the colleges — perhaps all of them, that was a viable build — As well as specialists who would decided, “I’m going to deep dive into just one or two colleges and that’s all. If I were going to make a future game, the two things I want to say is: I really like Will Wright’s rule of letting simple rules allow complexity to emerge. And I think that’s how I would put together a magic system in the future. I would make very simple rules for how magic worked And for each college or type of magic I’d make a very simple rule for how it worked and then they’d all follow that rule. But complexity would emerge because maybe spells could interact or maybe there were spell building blocks that you could combine that could interact. And, on that note, I really liked Tyranny’s spell building rules.
