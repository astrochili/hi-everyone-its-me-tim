So the advice I have is be very clear in your messaging and in all your messaging. Be consistent and be clear. You’re releasing a PR statement. Read it. Make sure it’s saying exactly what you want and not implying things you’re not giving. When you do interviews with game journalists, make sure you say the same things that were said in your PR statement.

Don’t promise things that. You’re not going to do. Don’t imply things thinking it makes the game sound better, that you know you’re not going to deliver.

And this is true whether you’re doing a conference, whether you’re, you know, doing some online Q&A on Reddit. Everywhere you should give the same message. Basically, what I recommend to people is State what kind of game you’re making. What the genre is what the setting is, what kind of story you’re telling. Talk about specifically about how many hours you expect people to get out of it and whether or not it’s replayable. And then — this is really important.

If you compare it to another game, say why you’re doing the comparison. Maybe they both are open world, maybe they share similar settings, maybe the humor style was the same. But be specific, because if you just say, hey, we’re a lot like, uh, halflife, people might expect an action. Shooter, people might expect an 80 hour game, people might expect the game to be in source. So there’s so many things that come when you make a comparison like that that you want to be very, very specific on what your comparison is.

Now I said on the one hand. The other hand is this: people are still going to think what they want. You can say all you want, you can do all you want, you can show clips from the game.

It’s not going to change some people’s minds. They’re still going to think what they think about your game and then make a comparison between what you said you were going to give and what you gave to what they thought you were going to do, and if that comparison is lacking, they are going to go online and they’re going to complain about it. Even though no one said it had that on the team, they expected it and then, when they didn’t get it, they got upset. That is exactly the kind of expectations that you want to try to manage. However, all the management in the world isn’t necessarily going to work, so you also have to accept, just like with discoverability, that some people who would love your game will never find it. You’re going to accept that some people who you think are right in your target audience, who learn about your game, will think you’re delivering something else, even though you didn’t say you were delivering that. And this has happened on every game I’ve ever had and I think you just have to find a way to manage that. Maybe you’re find a better way than I found to manage it. Um, so my final words on this: when you’re getting your game finished towards the end, really work hard on trying to manage the expectations of what game you’re delivering to the consumer. Try to talk directly to players in your interviews and go: here’s what we’re giving you, here’s what we think it’s like, here’s how long it’ll be, here’s what you should expect, and hopefully most people will hear you and there will be.
