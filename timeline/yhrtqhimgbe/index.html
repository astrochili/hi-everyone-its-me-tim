Hi everyone. It’s me, Tim. Today I want to talk about morality systems in RPGs.

This is actually a question from the discording tales 8198, who asks: what is your point of view on alignment or Karma systems in RPGs, systems of morality, of moralities, maybe, IE going Beyond good or evil, but not relativistic per se, using different sets of values measured from their own viewpoints? Hm, that is a bit of a vague question, um, but it’s also a difficult one, because now we’re talking, philosophy and definition of Good and Evil, and let me talk about what I tried in a couple games and then what I’m thinking now. So back in Fallout, we tried to do reputation and we just did kind of an overall reputation. So it wasn’t necessarily good or evil, it was. This was a positive thing to do towards your rep and this was a negative thing, and it was done via script and it turned out to be really varied, because what one group of people in on the team thought was a good thing for you to do for your reputation, other people thought was a bad thing. And in the game itself there was: hey, if you do this would be positive only for this one group. It’s a negative for this other group. So I don’t think Fallout was a good first step. It was a, mish mash of ideas. I mean, we had reputation titles in there too, like a champion of some group or you know, the child killer was a reputation title. So when we did Arcanum, I wanted to systematize these things.

Now it turned out you really can’t do that. So I’ll tell you what ended up happening. We ended up putting a script command in so that quests, when quests were completed or actions were taken by the player, those things could change alignment and we had good and evil and it was up to the person doing the quest or doing that script to decide if that was a good or evil act. Now, even dialogue could do that. You could say something, you could say a line, and it could be considered a really good thing to say or a really bad thing, because sometimes dialogue um had an action associated with it, like give, beggar money, um, and that was a good thing.

So that was still very, um, subjective. So I wanted something systemic, I wanted something built into the code that would change your alignment. What I wrote was this — and then I’ll tell you what shipped and I’ll tell you why, because I keep notes on everything — um, what I said was: whenever the player kills a good creature, anything whose alignment is greater than zero, that was an evil act. It doesn’t even matter if the player’s good or evil, doesn’t matter. If you kill something good, it’s an evil act and it pushes you towards Evil based on how good the thing you killed was. So killing an angel is a lot worse than killing a, cute little bunny.

But the other side was more complicated. If you kill an evil creature, it’s a good act if you were less evil than the creature, but it was an evil act if you were more evil. So basically, you have to think of it that one was relative.

If you killed something evil and you’re good, a good person, that was a good thing to do. If you kill something evil that’s far more evil than you, that’s also a good thing to do. You reduce the level of evil in the world. But if you are really, really evil and you take out something that’s just slightly evil, that’s not good.

Um, you’ve. I even said that’s more evil you’re basically taking out something that’s has more good in it than you have, so you’re reducing that amount of good. Now we capped how far that shift could happen in code, so you can’t become more good or evil than your T. Your victim was evil or good and I thought that would work and we had that.

Remember, we started Arcanum in 1998. We had it like that until February 2nd 2001, and I have a note that reads — and I will I’ll quote it reads, as per Jason Anderson on 2201: only allow negative alignment changes via script, um, I’m sorry, only allow negative alignment changes from actions. You can only become good through quests.

Now, that’s what the note says. What that means is if you — the systemic stuff allows you to get evil um go negative, which means if, you attack something good, you can still go negative. Um, or even if you attack something um less evil than you, go negative, but all the good changes do not occur.

They it’s an early out in the code. Now, the reason we did that turned out to be a balance issue, as we finished up most of the world, and the balance issue was this: when you’re just walking along, you tend to be attacked by evil things. When you go in the Dungeons and caves, they tend to be filled with evil things. When you walk around a town of good and evil people, you tend not to be attacked. The overall systemic effect was you were killing a lot more evil things than good things. So it was very easy to become good and stay good just by running around and killing, monsters.

So I know Jason had brought it up and we talked about it and we decided, yeah, that does seem to be. Um, it’s easy to be, it’s easier to become and stay good, because if you do something really horribly negative, you can just go out and kill some monsters and, boom, you’re good again. So we took that out and I was sad because I really really want a systemic good and evil system. But let’s take a look at what we learned from all that — and we talked about this a lot.

First of all, it’s hard to find good and evil. People on the team don’t agree. Um, philosophers don’t agree. I mean, the whole point of utilitarianism is you do things that make more good, but it’s hard to Define what good and evil are. And it’s also hard, not to say like it’s hard to live — to always do good. Things, because sometimes you just want to do something that’s nice, and nice isn’t always good. An example was: would you kill one person in order to definitely save the life of a 100, the classic trolley problem, and that just makes it Go: wow, there’s something going on here besides good and evil.

So we haven’t done much. I haven’t done meters like that since the closest I’ve gotten in subsequent games, um things like pillars, um of Eternity. We ended up doing a reputation by faction, and it wasn’t so much good and evil as it was like and dislike. If you’re do, if you do an action, what will the factions, different factions — think of it? Well, some like what you did, some hate what you did, and it’s usually pretty easy to decide that because you’ve made, you’ve done all these NPC factions and you’ve given them. Points of view, for example, stealing things — especially if you steal things when you’re a member of the Thieves Guild and come and sell them on their black market, they like that, but the T, the town, won’t like that. So you can easily see that stealing this item is a positive reputation for the Thieves Guild and a negative reputation for the town you stole it in.

And meanwhile there are other people who just wouldn’t care. They, don’t know anything about that item, they don’t know anything about that town, they’re not affected.

So doing reputation meters by faction seem to be pretty good at keeping track of these things. Another huge bonus is the way work tends to get broken down in games. Is there’s a person who, a designer, who made that faction and made the Quest for that faction and made the story for that faction? So even though two designers may agree or disagree on what a good, bad action is for a particular faction, there’s usually a designer who’s the final word on: yes, they like when you do this and they hate when you do this, so that that really works. I know there are games that do that on individual companions, that this companion likes this and doesn’t like this, and people have had all kinds of reaction towards that kind of reputation system for a companion.

But at the very minimum I I can say at least it a works. It works in a way you understand and you know what to do going forward and what cause it will have. If you’ve ever played a game, even a really good game, that has consequences for your actions that were completely unforeseeable, you have no idea until you do it either leads you to reloading old save games or looking everything up online. NPC asks you to do something in dialogue and you’re like: pause, let me Google that. I want to see you know, if I say yes, if I agree, what will happen? If I say no and disagree, what will happen?

So I I think where I landed by the end of my career was. I don’t like morality systems at all. What I like are reputations by factions based on whether or not whatever the action in question is something that faction would like you to do or not like you to do. Every faction, and sometimes a companion, can be its own faction — has its own set of likes and dislikes and the consequences of those. If some group really doesn’t like you, spend more money at their Merchants. If they really really dislike you, they don’t they comment and they don’t want you. Around, if they super dislike you, maybe it does become they attack you. And the opposite is, if they like you, cheaper prices, they offer more services. Maybe they give you quests to do. They like you, so I lean more towards that kind of thing, because once you go down the path of defining a morality, you’re going to find situations that you can’t figure out, situations that your team disagrees on and situations that game players disagree on, and then that leads to people reviewing your game going: I couldn’t figure out what I was supposed to do. I don’t know why this. You know Druid liked me doing this and why this cyborg hated me doing this.

I’m keeping it vague, just you end up with people saying that and it’s frustrating. So if you keep it to reputation by faction, I think you get a lot of what you want, which is the game, watching what you’re doing and reacting to it, but in a way that’s easier for the players to understand. So that’s my thought on moralities in games.
