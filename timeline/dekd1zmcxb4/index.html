That is a very good question, Phil, because I’m always surprised at what people think they wanted a producer, or even that they don’t know what a producer is. The producer is, he’s kind of at the top of the chain. Most people think the game director is, but in some ways the producer is because they’re handling everything that’s going on and making sure it’s getting done and interfacing with the publisher and interfacing with localization, interfacing with external groups that are doing work you may have, you know, maybe you’ve externed some of your art. They’re handling all that stuff, even if they have assistant producers who are directly handling it. They’re managing those people, so producers do a lot of stuff and you may think the answer I would give you is: I like a producer that does what I say. No, producers need to be a little tough because they work with all kinds of different people and they need to. They’re the ones who often put their foot down and say this has to be done or it won’t go into the game. Yeah, the game director does that a lot, but sometimes the producers — the one that even has to tell the game director you’re out of time, you’re out of money, or that person isn’t available and anymore they’re moving on to something else.

So either that feature — either you’re happy with that feature or we’re cutting that feature — decide so. If you don’t think you could do that, you may not be a good producer. But let me talk. I’m going to walk through. I think, five things. A producer has to be good at. Just to number them. Um, the first one may seem obvious. I’m going to say good communication, but let me tell you how hard that is.

In game development. You have to have good communication skills, even with people who don’t have good communication skills. There’s a lot of naturally introverted people in the game industry and you will have to learn to deal with all the different flavors of introversion that, come. You will have. You will run into people — and these are people I’ve run into myself — who never want you to come in and talk to them. Them if you look on their desk and they don’t have a little thing hanging on their monitor. But if that thing’s hanging on the monitor, that means don’t come in and bother me, even if they sometimes forget to take that little thing off their monitor. So all day you don’t talk to them because they’ve had that thing on the monitor.

You just have to figure out how to work with all these people. And you may think some of this is just: people are just getting so full of themselves and, oh, you have to accommodate me, I have to, you have to accommodate me, I don’t need to accommodate you, but it’s not. There’s some of that, but it’s not all that. I’ll give you an example.

I’ve talked about how, when I program, I often have this big structure. I’m building in my head and I’m converting. That’s the origin of why I started putting a comment, um, zzz in my code and then I realized whenever I got interrupted, um, and I put a zzz later, I’d find bugs around. Bugs were always within a few lines of a zzz. So it’s not necessarily it’s a personality type.

It’s the work I’m doing when I’m design, designing. You can probably interrupt me if I’m programming. I’d prefer you not, but there it has. It’s on me to let people know when and how they can come ask me questions. I can’t just say: never come talk to me.

You have to work out a system that works for you, and a producer is often the person you work that system out with. So, number one a: producer has to be a good communicator. And keep in mind, you’ll be communicating with not just devs, but with Publishers, with press, with manag at the company. You will be dealing with a whole bunch of people in a whole bunch of roles and you’ve got to be good at all of it because you’re a producer. If that’s what you want to do, that’s what you’re going to do. So that’s the first one. Good communication is the first one.

The second one is being organized. More than probably anyone else on the team, you have to be highly organized because game development these days, there’s a lot of moving Parts, there’s a lot of half finished things going into the game, some because they’ve been asked: hey, put in what you’ve got, we can at least use it until it gets textured. Sometimes half finish things go in because they literally want to test it. Hey, we’ve got it to a testable State. It’s not done but we can see how it works. You’ve got to track that it’s, in the game but not completed. Meanwhile you have to track all the things that are in pipelines that are partially completed. And because these days any particular feature or even asset that goes in the game isn’t done by one person, you got to track that and you got to know where everything is. And you have to know when you’re approaching some kind of deadline, not a deadline set by a, director, or a deadline set. By a publisher, a deadline that you yourself has realized is happening — because if something isn’t taken to a certain completion state in time for someone else to take over and do this and then someone else to take over and do their thing, you won’t be done in time for a deadline that’s months off. So it seems weird that you’re going to someone, going you have to be done Friday, even though the deadline for this thing maybe next month, and it’s because there’s a lot of people who need to use that feature or that asset after that person, and you’re the one who knows it. You can’t expect everyone working on it to know that, but the producer has to know it. They have to be the most organized person on the project.

So that’s the second thing. The third thing is they do have to be knowledgeable. On gamedev, I’ve met some producers who don’t think that’s true and have said things like: well, we might as well be making shoes. No, you need to know how Game Dev works and I’ll give you an example: making 3D assets in today’s Games. First it’s modeled. This is what 3D pipeline is, first it’s modeled — bunch of polygons shaped in a 3D software package — then it’s textured, then it’s rigged, um, which means bones are added if needed, and then it’s animated.

At each one of those stages a lot of design has to be known. The modeler has to know what it’s supposed to, how big it’s supposed to be and roughly shaped like and exactly shaped. And then the text needs to know what is it supposed to look like and what environments are. Is it in and is it supposed to stand out or is it supposed to blend in? And then the rigger needs. To know what parts of it need to move. Does it need to? You might have a creature that doesn’t need to be rigged around its face as much as a person, because it’s not expected to make expressions. And then you have something else.

It’s like: no, I want this to be highly expressable. And then, finally, you have an animator who needs to know who gets all these things and needs to know what animations are needed, because maybe this thing doesn’t have a death animation, it’s never going to be attacked, or maybe it needs multiple death animations, depending on what damage type is used to kill it. All these things have to be known by the time they get to that stage in the 3D pipeline. But here’s a kicker: can any of those things be done at the same time? Once the model’s done, can the one person texture while another? Person rigs it? Producer needs to know that, because you need to know, if you look on a on a schedule and you see rigging and texturing happening on the same model at the same time, that, if that’s possible and if not, you’re the one who brings up. Hey, I think this is a problem now, yes, the lead artist or your art director should have caught that too, but the producers — ultimately the one who makes and manages the schedule — they need to know that.

So, knowledgeable and Game Dev is number three. The fourth one is being proactive. I have a whole video on how people should be proactive. But wow, is that important for a producer? They have to be good at figuring them out and they have to be good at enforcing them. And this means the producer — and this goes back to communication — they’ll be looking at deadlines that publisher set, because here’s how much money you have, or here’s when we expect to have a demo, or here’s when we want to see a vertical slice. That’s an internal deadline that nobody will impose on you, but a producer is expected to figure out and set. So good at making, setting and enforcing deadlines is number five. I think, if you think you’ll be good at those five, you’d be.
