The problem is — I’ve talked at length about why this is a bad way of thinking, uh, and for a couple reasons and, by the way, all the videos will be linked in the description. The first reason this is bad is it completely ignores player agency. If a narrative designer approaches their characters as in terms of this will happen, then this will happen. They are completely disregarding that the player may not make those things happen, the player may not do any of those things. And then when you combine that with another topic I’ve talked about at length, nonlinearity — that the player may not do ABC, but they may do BAC or CBA, and this is something that your characters have to react to. You need to expect this plan for it and react to it.

This is something that would never happen in a book, never happen in a movie. The viewer of a movie, has no agency over the plot. The viewer of a movie can’t make the scenes go in a different order than the director, uh, or the editor finally made the movie go in. So this is a huge difference between games and movies. So because of that, I find that you can’t take a lot of the advice that you get from workshops and established authors of books and screenplays, because the recommendation is completely antithetical to what you’re going to have to do in games.

I’ll give you an example. Let’s say: well, a, common recommendation that I’ve heard about, for that works in book, books and movies, is: if you can remove a scene and it doesn’t affect the story, then that scene is not necessary. Because of player agency. You don’t know if a particular character is going to be important to them, if it is going to be a devastating blow to their playthrough. So one last thing I want to say about offering edits to a game storyline. Uh, maybe a few things. Um one: it is really easy to be negative when you’re writing down your suggestions for storyline edits, and the reason for that is you’re usually looking for problems. So when you look for problems, that means you’re pointing them out.

It’s going to sound negative. This is something I’ve had to explain to some of my clients in the last few years when they ask me to professionally review their game — and this has happened a lot — where they’re like, hey, we’ve got a vertical slice or we’ve got an alpha, we’ve got a prototype, and I’m like, okay, I tell them ahead of time, there is going to be a tendency to see what I’m giving you as negative, because you’ve asked me: is there anything in here that needs to change? You’re not asking me to validate how great your game is. You’re effectively asking me to go: did we do everything right? And if my answer is no, I’ve got to tell you what I think you’ve done wrong. That’s going to come across as a little negative. My suggestion here — and I have a video about that — is to always make sure that your review contains constructive Solutions.

Don’t just say, God, your characters are written terribly, say, hey, I’ve noticed your characters have a tendency to behave this way or treat the character this way, which makes me not want to interact with them, which led me to trying to do other things and then being forced to interact with them and being frustrated. Some of this is very subjective, but it’s I’ve talked about almost everything about making a game is subjective, but if you can make your solution constructive, hey, could you have a character that isn’t sarcastic? Could you have a character that isn’t nasty to me?

That’s enjoyable to interact with. You may find that there’s a lot more people who like playing your game. You don’t have to do this as long as you have enough variety that I think one of the solutions would work for me in my play style. But just by offering constructive Solutions may get my client to think of other solutions that I didn’t recommend that are similar to the ones I recommended.
