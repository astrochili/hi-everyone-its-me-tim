Hi everyone. It's me, Tim.

What you are seeing is the only known video footage of Epic, which was Troika in-house post-apocalyptic game we started designing and implementing towards the end of Vampire. Uh, of course it never came out. This footage isn't from mine. I'll include the link below. It's not from my archive. I don't have any video footage. In fact, I don't even have any code. I have a lot of design docs and notes, but my Epic folder seems very sparse.

The first document I have for this game is dated January of 2003. And the latest one is dated June of 2004, so let me tell you how I remember this, and I'll also tell you one thing that someone at least disagrees with me on. But so in 2003 we were contacted by Interplay who told us that Bethesda was bidding on the Fallout IP. And did we want to bid? We did. This is where our memories diverge. I remember contacting a friend who had venture capitalists who told me he could, um, guarantee a certain amount of money and I thought we bid. That Leonard Burarski says he remembers us saying we were going to bid but we didn't actually have the money set up either way.

Interplay took that, told Bethesda: hey, there's another bid. I don't know if they told him who it was from. I wasn't there. I, I don't think they did. Bethesda upped their bid to something higher than we could afford and the rest is history.

Bethesda got the Fallout license — license — license. Since we had this engine, we thought we'd do something with it. After Vampire: The Masquerade, the, um, the engine that we created was a natural extension of what we had done. Arcanum sprites on a tiled background, Lord of the Rings was sprites on a pre-rendered background, and Temple of Elemental Evil was 3D characters on a pre-rendered background. The engine we made for Epic was completely 3D but it was going to be isometric.

We made this engine ourselves. We had it from the game Fallout number four, but by the time Vampire was finished we knew the Source engine well. So we actually made multiple proposals for the same setting and story but on different engines: one for our engine, one for the Source engine.

I can tell you a little bit about that from the design docs. We had imagined a whole new world with post-apocalyptic and we weren't going to tell you what happened. We knew what happened. You would find evidence of what had happened when you play. All you know is the world had been wrecked centuries before and you don't know how. You lived in a world that had a ring and multiple moons, crazy tides, crazy monsters everywhere.

The world was basic, the world that you were playing basically had four groups from the four compass points. To the north were barbarians coming out of a cold waste. To the east was an empire that still maintained some of the cities, but they were in ruins and they seemed to control magic now. Is it magic? Is it attack? I don't know, seems like magic. To the west was a vast desert of ruined cities, rusty girders and cement pylons, and the people over there were called the Wardens of Rust. And to the south was a swamp filled with the Bogman. We, these people, were coming together into the center. They were fighting over territory there, and that's where you come in. You selected one of those groups to be from and you also selected a background: what you did in that group.

That combination of group and background determined your starting skill category. We had four different skill categories. We had social skills, combat skills, thieving skills, and magic skills. You started in those categories and you could only buy in those categories, unless you started buying crossover skills. Every pair of skills had crossovers. For example, um, pickpocketing or stealing might be in thieving, and there were combat melee and ranged skills in combat. But a crossover thieving combat skill would be intimidating. A combat or, yeah, crossover thieving social skill might be fast talk. So you could buy these crossover skills which combined the little elements of both, and then, once you had a crossover skill, you could then start buying stuff in the ability tree that you weren't either born into or grew up with, so you can make all kinds of different characters with different backgrounds.

They would just start different, and then you could go where you wanted. We also had stats that you got at the beginning. We had eight of them. They were pretty much standard stuff, um, those you got at the beginning and were fixed. The adventure we had planned was very open.

It was: what group did you help get control of that central region? Uh, I believe you could even go into the other regions, but they were firmly in control of those factions. There was a lot of, uh, discovery of strange items and artifacts gave clues onto what had happened in the time before. There were a lot of isolated groups that were not connected to any of these, um, people who claimed they had been around in the old world, that knew things that could give you knowledge and items if you would work for them. So we had this whole game planned out. Uh, we did run around and propose it a lot in late 2004, early 2005, didn't get any takers and we ended up closing Troika in February of 2005 and, for real, the final paperwork was August of 2005.

But Epic was interesting, it was. It was fun to work. It had been seven years since we had worked Fallout. It was fun to think about post-apocalyptic, again without the constraints. Just the same way, Fallout was fun to work without the constraints of Wasteland. Epic was fun to work without the constraints of Fallout. But it was nice to revisit post-apoc, do a different spin on it.

Nothing ever came of it. The video that is on YouTube is the only remaining, even video or even screenshot that I've seen and it's real.
